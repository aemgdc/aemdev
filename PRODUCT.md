# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

AEM developers and practitioners who follow or attend the AEM Global Developer Collective
meetups. They visit to catch up on what they missed — reading article deep-dives and
watching/reading meetup recaps relevant to their current AEM work. Secondary: AEM community
members discovering the group for the first time.

Context: they are mid-project or post-meetup, looking for specific, expert knowledge.
They scan fast and read deeply when something is relevant. They are skeptical of corporate
polish and respond to practitioner voice.

## Product Purpose

aemdev.org is the public home of the AEM Global Developer Collective — a practitioner-run
user group affiliated with Adobe AUGs. It hosts two content streams (Articles, Meetup Recaps)
and a minimal contact page.

Success: a developer who missed a meetup can find the recap in under 30 seconds, trust its
depth, and share it with a colleague. The site feels community-built, not corporate-deployed.

## Content Character

### Articles

Technical depth is the norm. Many articles will have little or no photography — a code
walkthrough, an architecture decision, an AEM API explainer. These pages must still be
visually engaging: **every article gets a designed hero image** (typographic, geometric,
or abstract-technical in style) rather than relying on photos. The design system must make
it easy to produce compelling hero treatments for text-heavy content.

### Meetup Recaps

Rich media is the norm. Recaps frequently include photos of attendees and speakers at
in-person or virtual events, video recordings, and slide embeds. **Community imagery is a
first-class design element** — photos of AEM developers at conferences and meetups should
feel featured, not incidental. The design must make it trivially easy to showcase a gallery
of event photos alongside a video embed and written summary.

## Brand Personality

**Three words:** Expert. Energized. Open.

Voice: peer-to-peer, direct, technically serious but not austere. Writes like someone who
has shipped AEM for years and is excited to share what they learned — not like a vendor
writing a whitepaper.

Emotional goal: builder pride. Visiting the site should feel like joining a group of people
who actually build things with AEM and care about doing it well. Community photos of real
developers at real events reinforce this — this is a place made by and for practitioners.

## References

- **Linear.app blog / Changelog.com** — clean, high-craft, not shy about dark themes.
  Precise but warm. Developer-culture premium without feeling cold.
- **TLDR newsletter / developer.adobe.com** — no-frills but curated. Respects the reader's
  time. Slightly utilitarian; density is a feature, not a bug.

## Anti-references

- **Adobe.com marketing pages** — polished, impersonal, sales-oriented. This site is
  by practitioners for practitioners. No hero copy that sounds like a product launch.
  No stock photography. No "Transform your digital experience" language.

## Design Principles

1. **Practitioners over pageantry.** Content and community are the product. Chrome,
   decoration, and motion exist only when they serve reading or navigation.

2. **Every page earns its visual weight.** Articles without photos get designed hero
   treatments — typographic, geometric, or abstract — so visual quality is never
   contingent on having a photo. Photography exists in abundance for events; use it.

3. **Community is visible.** Event photos of real AEM developers at real meetups are
   featured deliberately, not tucked away. The "people" layer of this community should
   be as visible as the technical content layer.

4. **Curated density.** Like TLDR and Changelog — give readers useful information without
   wasting their attention. Cards, lists, and headlines carry full weight; no filler copy.

5. **Adobe Red with restraint.** Use the brand color to signal the AEM ecosystem
   connection — one strong accent, not a red takeover. The color earns attention;
   it does not demand it.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Best-effort accessibility — aim for WCAG 2.1 AA contrast ratios on primary text and
interactive elements, keyboard navigability on all interactive elements, and `alt` text
on all images. No hard compliance target, but do not knowingly break accessibility.
Community event photos must have descriptive alt text (people and context, not just "photo").
